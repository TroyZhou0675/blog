Christmas is drawing near. When you think of a Christmas hero, who do you think of? Santa Claus? Maybe a snowman? Well, if you go to Japan, the hero of Christmas is a little different. He has white hair. He has a white beard. But… he doesn’t ride a sleigh. He fries chicken. Yes! I am talking to the KFC Grandpa. In Japan, KFC is the King of Christmas. Today, I want to tell you about the funny differences between Christmas in the East and the West, based on some interesting cultural research I gathered recently.

The first big difference is what we eat. In the West, the star of the show is the Turkey. But let’s be honest—cooking a turkey is a nightmare. It takes five hours, the bird is massive, and the result? It usually tastes like paper! But in Asia, Japan found a better way. According to **Smithsonian Magazine [1]** and **BBC Worklife [2]**, this tradition started in 1974 with a marketing campaign called “Kentucky for Christmas.” The story goes that a KFC manager heard foreigners in Tokyo complaining that they couldn’t find turkey, so he offered fried chicken as a substitute. It worked perfectly. Even today, 50 years later, **CNN Travel reports [3]** that an estimated 3.6 million Japanese families treat themselves to KFC on Christmas. To put it simply: For them, Christmas is basically just the ultimate 'Crazy Thursday'!

The second big difference is about what we do. I know there is a debate about this: Is Christmas for family or for lovers? Some might argue that in the US, Thanksgiving is the main reunion holiday. **Pew Research Center [4]** data shows that family gathering is still the priority for Americans on Christmas, but let's look at Europe. In the UK and most of Europe, Thanksgiving doesn't exist. For them, and for most Western families in general, Christmas remains the undisputed time for going home, as noted in British historical records **[5]**.

But in East Asia—in China, Japan, Korea—Christmas is clearly for Lovers. It is basically a second Valentine’s Day. If you look at social trends in Tokyo or Seoul, restaurants are fully booked by couples, not big families **[6]**. Have you heard the song by Eason Chan, "Lonely Christmas"? It perfectly captures this vibe. If you are single, you don't go home to grandma; you go out to party with friends.

Now, you might ask: Why? Why is it so different? **According to cultural researchers Kimura and Belk [7]**, the answer lies in the calendar. They explain that in East Asia, the **New Year** (or Spring Festival) is the real "Sacred Family Time." Because we already have a heavy, traditional holiday for family reunions, Christmas became a "secular vacuum." Since we don't have the pressure of religious rules or family duties on December 25th, we turned it into a day purely for fun, romance, and consumption.

In the end, the world is a funny place. On one side, we have dry turkey and loud families. On the other side, we have romantic dates and a bucket of KFC fried chicken. But you know what? It doesn't matter if you eat turkey or chicken. It doesn't matter if you are with your grandma or your boyfriend. For us, Christmas is just about being happy. So, I hope you all have a wonderful Christmas. And remember: keep it "Finger Lickin’ Good." Thank you!
