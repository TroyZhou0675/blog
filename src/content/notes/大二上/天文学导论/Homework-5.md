## Question 1

**(1)What are the three major stellar structures in the Milky Way Galaxy? Could you order them from the center outward?**
The Milky Way Galaxy is comprised of three primary stellar structures, which, when ordered from the galactic center outward, are the **bulge**, the **disk**, and the **halo**.

**(2)Which component contains most of the stars in the Milky Way? What matter in the Milky Way contains most of the gravitational matter?**
The **disk** is the component that contains the vast majority of the Milky Way's stars. However, the matter that accounts for most of the gravitational mass in the Milky Way is not stars but **dark** **matter**.

**(3)What is the ratio of the mass in this gravitationally dominant matter to the total mass in stars?**
Dark matter is estimated to constitute about 90% of the total mass of the Milky Way. This leads to a ratio of dark matter to stellar mass of approximately 10 to 1.

**(4)How was this gravitationally dominant matter discovered in Milky Way?**
The existence of this gravitationally dominant dark matter in the Milky Way was first inferred from the galaxy's rotation curve. Astronomers observed that the orbital speeds of stars and gas clouds in the outer regions of the galaxy did not decrease with distance from the galactic center as would be expected based on the visible matter alone. Instead, these rotational velocities remained surprisingly constant, or "flat." This discrepancy implied the presence of a massive, unseen halo of matter exerting a strong gravitational pull, which is now known as dark matter.

## Question 2
**We see a lot of dark patches along the Milky Way disk through optical telescopes on the ground or in space. Do these regions happen to have fewer stars? Electromagnetic waves, i.e., photons, can be absorbed or scattered by dust . In order for the absorption or scattering to be effective, should dust grains be smaller or larger than the wavelengths of the photons? Please explain.**

The dark patches prominent in optical views of the Milky Way's disk are not regions devoid of stars. On the contrary, these dark lanes are teeming with stars, but their light is obscured from our view. These areas are vast clouds of interstellar dust and gas, so dense that they block the visible light from the stars located behind them. Known as dark nebulae or molecular clouds, these cosmic dust clouds are not empty voids but are, in fact, crucial stellar nurseries where new stars are born, hidden from optical telescopes within their dusty cocoons.

For the absorption or scattering of electromagnetic waves, such as light photons, to be effective, the dust grains must be comparable in size to, or larger than, the wavelength of the light. This relationship between the size of a particle and the wavelength of light determines the nature and efficiency of their interaction. If the dust particles were significantly smaller than the wavelength of the light, the light waves would mostly pass by them undisturbed, much like an ocean wave passes by a small pebble with little effect.

The reason for this is rooted in the physics of light interaction. When a photon's wavelength is much larger than a dust grain, the light wave can diffract, or bend, around the tiny particle without significant absorption or scattering. However, when the size of the dust grain is similar to or exceeds the wavelength, the grain presents a substantial obstacle to the light wave. This leads to a much higher probability of the photon being either absorbed by the grain, converting its energy into heat, or scattered, changing its direction of travel. This latter effect is what makes the clouds appear opaque.

This principle explains why we observe the dark patches in the Milky Way. The dust grains that populate the interstellar medium have sizes that are very effective at blocking shorter wavelengths of light, such as the blue and green light our eyes are most sensitive to. Consequently, these dust clouds form an impenetrable wall for visible light, creating the dark rifts we see. However, longer wavelengths, such as infrared and radio waves, are much larger than the dust grains and can pass through these clouds with less interference, allowing astronomers to use specialized telescopes to peer inside these dark nebulae and study the hidden processes of star formation.
