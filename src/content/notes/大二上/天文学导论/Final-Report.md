# The Invisible Grip : How we discovered Dark matter through its gravitational effects

Name：周祎彤
Number:3240100635

In the vast universe, all the objects that we can see with the naked eyes, from brilliant stars to the huge galaxies and nebulae, actually only consists of  5% of the universe's mass. That means, the majority of the universe is invisible to us. In the early 20 century, the astronomers faced this problem,  called "Missing Mass Problem". They  calculated the total mass of the galaxies and star clusters, finding them much bigger than the total mass of all the visible objects like stars and nebulae. The huge difference indicated that, there must exist some invisible matter that does not interact with the electromagnetic force but have huge gravitational effects. This is what we call-the dark matter. The report will explain how we through two important pieces of evidence --galaxy rotation curves and gravitational lensing, to find this invisible matter.

The first line of evidence comes from observing the motion of stars in spiral galaxies.  According to the Newtonian Gravity ,if we assume most of a galaxy's mass is concentrated in its visible center, we could expect that the star's orbital velocity decreases as its distance from the center increases. This is called the Keplerian Decline. However,  when astronomers like Vera Rubin measured the the velocities of those stars around the galaxy, they found a shocking result: the velocity didn't decrease when the distance form the center grew. Instead, it remained almost constant. This observed curves is called the "Flat Rotation Curve", and this phenomenon is utterly contrast to our expectation. Thus, the only explanation is, apart from those luminous matters, there exists a huge Dark Matter Halo. The mass of this halo is much bigger than the total mass of the stars and gases in the galaxy, providing extra gravitational force needed to keep outer stars moving at such a high velocity.

The second line of evidence is based on the General Relativity raised by the Albert Einstein. The theory predicted that, objects with big mass can bend spacetime around, when the light go through this spacetime, its route will be deflected, just like a flash of light goes through a lens. This phenomenon  is called the "Gravitational Lending". Galaxy clusters are the largest gravitational bond structure in the universe. They can act like a huge lens to distort and amplify the light from distant galaxies behind them, and even forming multiple images. Through measuring the degree of the distortion, astronomers can precisely "weigh out" the total mass of the galaxy. To everyone's surprise: the mass measured by the gravitational lensing is 5 to 10 times of the mass of its visible matters. This indicates that most of the mass in the galaxy cluster comes from dark matter.

There is an example called "Bullet Cluster", providing the most direct evidence yet for the existence of dark matter. The "Bullet Cluster" actually is the result of the collision of two galaxy clusters. In the collision, the Baryonic Matter comprised of common matters will slow down from interaction. Through the gravitational lens, we made the distribution graph of the system, finding the center of the mass is separated from the center of the common gas. The center of gravity is exactly where those two invisible clumps of dark matter are. This separation of "visible matter" and "gravity center" is considered to be "Smoking Gun Evidence" that proves the real existence of dark matter.

According to the report, two pieces of evidence from separate fields all point to the same conclusion:  There is a invisible substance in our universe that dominate the evolution of universe through gravity. Those overwhelming evidence has made the dark matter from a theoretical assumption to the cornerstone of the Standard Model of Modern Cosmology. Although we haven't detected the dark matter particle in the earth's labs, and haven't determined its basic properties. But its "fingerprints" left by gravity are spread throughout the entire universe. Thus unveiling the mystery of the dark matter is not only the key of understanding how galaxies were formed, but also the ultimate challenge of improving our understanding of the fundamental composition of the universe.
